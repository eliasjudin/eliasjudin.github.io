Symmetries in Algebra

04 May 2025

Algebraic categories come with intrinsic symmetries and structural constraints that control how far homological methods extend. This note follows one thread through that landscape: automorphisms of categories of RR-algebras, representable functors and their coalgebra structure, protomodularity and its equational test, and torsion theories leading to rings of quotients.

1. Automorphisms of algebraic categories

Let RR be a commutative ring with unit and consider the following familiar categories:

  • AR\mathbf A_R: associative RR-algebras (not necessarily unital);
  • AR1\mathbf A_R^1: unital associative RR-algebras with unit-preserving morphisms;
  • CR\mathbf C_R: commutative associative RR-algebras;
  • CR1\mathbf C_R^1: commutative unital RR-algebras.

Write Aut⁡(C)\operatorname{Aut}(\mathcal C) for the group of equivalence classes of auto-equivalences of a category C\mathcal C; composition gives the group structure. Any ring automorphism φ∈Aut⁡(R)\varphi \in \operatorname{Aut}(R) induces an auto-equivalence

Iφ ⁣:A⟼A with r⋅IφAa:=φ(r)a, I_\varphi\colon A \longmapsto A\ \text{with } r \cdot_{I_\varphi A} a := \varphi(r) a,

which is natural in AA and preserves units when they exist. Thus there is an anti-homomorphism Aut⁡(R)→Aut⁡(C)\operatorname{Aut}(R) \to \operatorname{Aut}(\mathcal C) (equivalently, a homomorphism Aut⁡(R)op→Aut⁡(C)\operatorname{Aut}(R)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \operatorname{Aut}(\mathcal C)) for each of the four categories above.

When multiplication in the objects is non-commutative, there is an additional order-two symmetry: the opposite-algebra functor

(−)op ⁣:A⟼Aop,a∗Aopb:=ba. (-)^{\mathrm{op}}\colon A \longmapsto A^{\mathrm{op}}, \qquad a *_{A^{\mathrm{op}}} b := ba.

It commutes with every IφI_\varphi. Consequently, the assignment (φ,ϵ)↦Iφ∘((−)op)ϵ(\varphi,\epsilon) \mapsto I_\varphi \circ ((-)^{\mathrm{op}})^{\epsilon} yields an injective homomorphism

Aut⁡(R)op×C2↪Aut⁡(AR), \operatorname{Aut}(R)^{\mathrm{op}} \times C_2 \hookrightarrow \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbf A_R),

where C2={0,1}C_2 = \{0,1\} and ((⋅)op)0=Id((\cdot)^{\mathrm{op}})^0=\mathrm{Id}, ((⋅)op)1=(−)op((\cdot)^{\mathrm{op}})^1=(-)^{\mathrm{op}}. The same construction yields an embedding Aut⁡(R)op×C2↪Aut⁡(AR1)\operatorname{Aut}(R)^{\mathrm{op}} \times C_2 \hookrightarrow \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbf A_R^1). In the commutative categories the opposite functor is naturally isomorphic to the identity, so we still obtain an embedding

Aut⁡(R)op↪Aut⁡(CR),and likewise Aut⁡(R)op↪Aut⁡(CR1). \operatorname{Aut}(R)^{\mathrm{op}} \hookrightarrow \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbf C_R), \qquad \text{and likewise } \operatorname{Aut}(R)^{\mathrm{op}} \hookrightarrow \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbf C_R^1).

Under additional preservation hypotheses (for instance, requiring an auto-equivalence to carry free algebras to free algebras and to commute with the forgetful functor up to isomorphism), these are the symmetries that occur in practice. The absence of the (−)op(-)^{\mathrm{op}} symmetry in the commutative setting reflects a general principle: categorical properties must be invariant under every auto-equivalence. “Having a left identity” fails to be categorical in AR\mathbf A_R because it is not preserved by (−)op(-)^{\mathrm{op}}; commutativity is categorical precisely because the functor acts trivially there.

2. Representable functors and coalgebra structure

Functors mediate between algebraic worlds, and representable functors make that mediation concrete. In any finitary variety V\mathcal V, the underlying-set functor U ⁣:V→SetU\colon \mathcal V\to\mathbf{Set} is represented by the free object on one generator F(1)F(1)1:

Hom⁡V(F(1),B)≅U(B)=∣B∣. \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal V}(F(1), B) \cong U(B) = |B|.

This isomorphism is natural in BB. Dualising the universal property endows F(1)F(1) with a canonical coalgebra structure: the “cooperations” Δω ⁣:F(1)→∐i=1nF(1)\Delta_\omega\colon F(1) \to \coprod_{i=1}^n F(1) (the coproduct of nn copies of F(1)F(1)) encode how each basic operation ω ⁣:Bn→B\omega\colon B^{n}\to B is recovered from the representing object. Equivalently, using that the free functor preserves coproducts, one may view Δω\Delta_\omega as a map F(1)→F(n)F(1)\to F(n) via the canonical identification F(n)≅∐i=1nF(1)F(n)\cong \coprod_{i=1}^n F(1). The pair (F(1),(Δω))(F(1), (\Delta_\omega)) retains the full algebraic theory; morphisms from F(1)F(1) measure elements, while the cooperations record how the theory reassembles them. This is the Lawvere‑theoretic viewpoint1. For instance, in associative unital RR-algebras one has F(1)=R⟨x⟩F(1)=R\langle x\rangle, and in commutative RR-algebras, F(1)=R[x]F(1)=R[x]. (For a self‑contained introduction to free objects and their universal property, see Free algebras by means of a universal property.)

3. Protomodular varieties and Mal’tsev terms

Protomodularity (in the sense of Bourn) captures a robust form of non‑abelian exactness: in a protomodular category, pullbacks of split epimorphisms are well behaved, and a Split Short Five Lemma holds. For varieties this interacts fruitfully with equational structure via Mal’tsev terms.

  • A variety is Mal’tsev if it admits a ternary term p(x,y,z)p(x,y,z) such that p(x,x,z)=zp(x,x,z)=z and p(x,y,y)=xp(x,y,y)=x. Groups have p(x,y,z)=xy−1zp(x,y,z)=xy^{-1}z; rings, modules, and Lie algebras have p(x,y,z)=x−y+zp(x,y,z)=x-y+z on the underlying additive groups. Mal’tsev varieties enjoy strong exactness properties2.

  • Every variety of Ω\Omega-groups (groups with additional operations that are homomorphisms in each variable—e.g. rings without unit and Lie algebras) is protomodular—and in fact semi‑abelian. Thus the familiar algebraic categories used in homological algebra fit squarely into the protomodular framework3.

These structural conditions guarantee that kernels, cokernels, and split extensions interact as expected, enabling a non‑abelian version of many abelian techniques (actions, internal semidirect products, and the like).

4. Torsion theories and rings of quotients

A torsion theory on Mod-R\mathrm{Mod}\text{-}R is a pair (T,F)(\mathcal T, \mathcal F) of full subcategories with Hom⁡R(T,F)=0\operatorname{Hom}_R(T,F)=0 for T∈TT \in \mathcal T and F∈FF \in \mathcal F, and such that each module MM fits into a natural short exact sequence 0→τ(M)→M→M/τ(M)→00\to \tau(M)\to M\to M/\tau(M)\to 0 with τ(M)∈T\tau(M)\in\mathcal T and M/τ(M)∈FM/\tau(M)\in\mathcal F. Equivalently, one specifies the torsion radical τ\tau (an idempotent preradical; τ\tau is left exact precisely for hereditary torsion theories) by the rule τ(M)⊆M\tau(M)\subseteq M. Dense submodules are those whose quotient is torsion; closed submodules are those whose quotient is torsion‑free4.

Beyond module categories, torsion theories admit a clean categorical formulation and interact tightly with closure operators and factorization systems. In a pointed category with a chosen ideal of null morphisms (equivalently, a full subcategory of null objects), Grandis–Janelidze show that suitable torsion data correspond to closure operators and, in a “morphism category”, to factorization structures. Concretely: closure operators encode which subobjects are “closed” (torsion‑free) under a given reflective subcategory, while factorization systems isolate the “torsion” vs “torsion‑free” parts of morphisms by stable (E,M)‑factorizations5. This perspective recovers the abelian case and explains why hereditary torsion theories in Mod-R\mathrm{Mod}\text{-}R yield localization functors.

Primer: a categorical closure operator assigns to every mono m ⁣:N↪Mm\colon N\hookrightarrow M a mono m‾ ⁣:N‾↪M\overline m\colon \overline N\hookrightarrow M with m≤m‾m\le \overline m, such that it is monotone in mm, stable under pullback, and idempotent (m‾‾=m‾\overline{\overline m}=\overline m). Subobjects fixed by closure are called closed; monos whose closure is an isomorphism are dense. In Mod-R\mathrm{Mod}\text{-}R for a torsion pair (T,F)(\mathcal T,\mathcal F) with radical τ\tau, the closure of a submodule N⊆MN\subseteq M is given explicitly by

N‾  =  π−1( τ(M/N) ),π ⁣:M↠M/N, \overline N\;=\;\pi^{-1}\big(\,\tau(M/N)\,\big), \qquad \pi\colon M\twoheadrightarrow M/N,

so that “dense” means M/NM/N is torsion and “closed” means M/NM/N is torsion‑free46.

Primer: a factorization system (E,M)(\mathcal E,\mathcal M) on a category consists of two classes of morphisms such that every arrow ff factors functorially as f=m ef = m\,e with e∈Ee\in\mathcal E, m∈Mm\in\mathcal M, and E=⊥ ⁣M\mathcal E = {}^{\perp}\!\mathcal M, M=E⊥\mathcal M = \mathcal E^{\perp} (orthogonality: each commutative square with left edge in E\mathcal E and right edge in M\mathcal M has a unique diagonal filler). The classes are closed under composition and contain all isomorphisms. Example: in Set\mathbf{Set}, (surjections, injections)(\text{surjections},\,\text{injections}) is an orthogonal factorization system7. For a localization LL (reflector into the torsion‑free subcategory), one obtains a canonical (E,M)(\mathcal E,\mathcal M) where E\mathcal E are the LL-inverted (“dense”) maps and M\mathcal M the LL-cartesian (“closed”) maps; in abelian settings such as Mod-R\mathrm{Mod}\text{-}R with a hereditary torsion pair the orthogonality conditions are satisfied, and this recovers the dense/closed behavior described above5.

The maximal right ring of quotients Qmax⁡(R)Q_{\max}(R) corresponds to the largest hereditary torsion theory with RRR_R torsion-free. Classical constructions use injective hulls and endomorphism rings to realise Qmax⁡(R)Q_{\max}(R) and relate it to dense submodules; see the references below. This perspective ties localisation back to intrinsic symmetries of endomorphism rings, closing the circle with the automorphism analysis that began the discussion.

All four themes reinforce each other: automorphisms act on representable functors, the coalgebra structure packages the operations of the theory, protomodularity supplies exactness principles, and torsion theories provide the local data underpinning these symmetries. The resulting picture is one where categorical symmetry, algebraic structure, and localisation form a single, coherent tapestry.
